Chapter 01

What Is Pokémon?

Pokémon (short for "Pocket Monsters") is a fictional universe created in Japan in 1996 by game designer Satoshi Tajiri and illustrator Ken Sugimori. In this world, humans known as Trainers capture and befriend creatures called Pokémon — each with unique abilities, elemental powers, and personalities.

There are over 1,000 different species of Pokémon. Some breathe fire, some control water, some create electricity, and many evolve into stronger forms as they grow. Think of it as a world where wildlife has superpowers and people build teams of them to compete in friendly battles.

The franchise spans video games, an animated TV series, movies, toys, and — most relevant to us — a Trading Card Game (TCG). In the card game, you play as a Pokémon Trainer, using a deck of cards to battle another Trainer's team.

Why People Love It

The TCG blends strategic depth with accessible rules. Building a deck is creative and personal — you choose which creatures and strategies represent you. Games are fast (usually 15–25 minutes) and every match unfolds differently based on what cards you draw and how you respond to your opponent.

Chapter 02

A Brief History

The Pokémon TCG launched in Japan on October 20, 1996, published by Media Factory and developed by Creatures Inc. It was built on the runaway success of the Pokémon Red and Green video games for the Game Boy. The first set — the "Base Set" — featured 102 cards with iconic creatures like Charizard, Blastoise, and Pikachu.

In January 1999, Wizards of the Coast (the company behind Magic: The Gathering) brought the card game to North America. Timing was perfect — "Pokémania" was at fever pitch with the animated TV series airing worldwide, and the card game became an instant cultural phenomenon.

Since then, the game has continuously evolved. New expansion sets, mechanics, and Pokémon are introduced regularly alongside each new generation of video games. As of 2025, over 75 billion cards have been produced worldwide in 16 languages, and the game is sold in over 90 countries. It remains one of the most popular trading card games ever created.

💡

The rarest Pokémon card is the "Pikachu Illustrator," originally created as a prize for a 1997 Japanese art contest. Only about 41 were ever printed, and one sold for $16.5 million in February 2026.

Chapter 03

The Three Types of Cards

Every 60-card deck is built from exactly three types of cards. Understanding what each does is the foundation of everything.

🐉

Pokémon Cards

Your battlers. Each has Hit Points (HP), one or more attacks, a type (like Fire or Water), and sometimes special Abilities. They come in three stages: Basic (played directly from your hand), Stage 1 (evolves from a Basic), and Stage 2 (evolves from a Stage 1). You must always have at least one Pokémon in play.

⚡

Energy Cards

The fuel for your Pokémon's attacks. You attach Energy cards from your hand to your Pokémon to power their moves. Each attack lists its Energy cost — you need to match those symbols. Basic Energy has no limit per deck. Special Energy provides extra effects but is limited to 4 copies.

🎒

Trainer Cards

Items, tools, and allies that support your strategy. They come in subtypes: Item cards (play as many as you want), Supporter cards (only one per turn — these are powerful), Stadium cards (one per turn — affects both players), and Pokémon Tools (attach to a Pokémon for ongoing effects).

Chapter 05

Reading a Pokémon Card

Every Pokémon card packs a lot of information into a small space. Here's what each part means, from top to bottom:

Top of the Card

Name — the Pokémon's name and any special designation (like "ex" or "V"). The Stage (Basic, Stage 1, Stage 2) is in the upper left. The HP (Hit Points) is in the upper right — this is how much damage the Pokémon can take before being Knocked Out. The Type symbol beside HP tells you the Pokémon's elemental type.

Middle of the Card

The card's artwork dominates the center. Below it, you'll find the Pokémon's Abilities (special powers that are always active or triggered without attacking) and its Attacks. Each attack shows: the Energy cost (symbols on the left), the attack name, the damage number (on the right), and a text description of any extra effects.

Bottom of the Card

Weakness — a type symbol showing which type deals double damage to this Pokémon. Resistance — a type symbol with "−30" showing which type deals reduced damage. Retreat Cost — the number of Energy you must discard from this Pokémon to swap it to your Bench. These three items appear in a row at the card's bottom.

Chapter 06

Setting Up the Game

Before the battle begins, both players prepare the field. Here's the step-by-step:

1

Shake Hands & Shuffle

Both players shuffle their 60-card decks thoroughly. A coin flip determines who goes first (the first player cannot attack on their first turn).

2

Draw 7 Cards

Each player draws 7 cards as their opening hand. Don't show your hand to your opponent.

3

Place Your Active Pokémon

Choose 1 Basic Pokémon from your hand and place it face-down in your Active Spot. If you have no Basic Pokémon, reveal your hand, shuffle it back, and draw 7 new cards. Your opponent gets to draw 1 extra card each time this happens.

4

Fill Your Bench (Optional)

You may place any additional Basic Pokémon from your hand face-down on your Bench (up to 5 Pokémon on the Bench).

5

Set Aside Prize Cards

Each player takes the top 6 cards of their deck and places them face-down to the side as Prize Cards. You don't look at these — they're your rewards for Knocking Out opposing Pokémon.

6

Flip Cards & Begin!

Both players flip their Pokémon face-up. The game starts — the first player draws a card and begins their turn.

Chapter 07

What You Do on Your Turn

Every turn follows three phases: Draw, Do Things, and Attack. The middle phase is where most of the strategy happens.

Phase 1 — Draw a Card

You must draw exactly one card from the top of your deck. This is mandatory. If your deck is empty and you can't draw, you lose the game immediately.

Phase 2 — Do Things (in any order)

This is the action phase. You can do any combination of the following, in any order:

ActionLimitDetails
Play Basic Pokémon to Bench As many as you want Place Basic Pokémon from your hand onto your Bench (max 5 Benched Pokémon)
Evolve Pokémon As many as you want Play a Stage 1 or Stage 2 on top of the matching Pokémon in play. Can't evolve a Pokémon on its first turn in play, or on your first turn of the game
Attach Energy Once per turn Take one Energy card from your hand and attach it to any of your Pokémon (Active or Benched)
Play Item Cards As many as you want Follow the card's instructions, then discard it
Play a Supporter Card Once per turn Powerful effects — only one Supporter per turn
Play a Stadium Card Once per turn Stays in play and affects both players. A new Stadium replaces the old one
Use Abilities As many as you want Some Pokémon have Abilities — special powers that aren't attacks. Read each card for details
Retreat your Active Pokémon Once per turn Discard Energy equal to the Retreat Cost, then swap in a Benched Pokémon

Phase 3 — Attack

Declare one of your Active Pokémon's attacks (if it has enough Energy attached). Apply the damage and any effects. Your turn is now over. You can't do any Phase 2 actions after attacking. If you can't or don't want to attack, your turn simply ends.

Chapter 08

How Attacking Works

Attacking is the climax of your turn. Here's how to resolve it step by step:

A

Check Energy

Make sure your Active Pokémon has enough Energy attached to pay for the attack you want to use. The Energy stays attached — it doesn't get used up (unless the attack says otherwise).

B

Check Weakness & Resistance

Look at the defending Pokémon (your opponent's Active). If it's Weak to your attacker's type, double the damage. If it's Resistant, subtract 30.

C

Place Damage Counters

Put damage counters on the defending Pokémon equal to the final damage (each counter = 10 HP). If total damage on a Pokémon equals or exceeds its HP, it's Knocked Out.

D

Knock Out & Prizes

When you Knock Out an opponent's Pokémon, it and all attached cards go to their discard pile. You take one of your Prize Cards into your hand. Your opponent must promote a Benched Pokémon to the Active Spot.

⚠️

Important: Only your Active Pokémon can attack, and it can only attack the opponent's Active Pokémon (unless an attack specifically says it targets the Bench). The first player of the game cannot attack on their very first turn.

Chapter 09

Weakness & Resistance

These are printed on each Pokémon card in the lower-left area. They add a layer of strategy based on type matchups — similar to how water puts out fire in the real world.

⚠️ Weakness (×2)

If your Pokémon has a Weakness to a type, attacks from Pokémon of that type deal double damage. For example, if a Grass-type Pokémon is weak to Fire, a Fire Pokémon's 60-damage attack would deal 120 damage instead. This is devastating and often results in a one-hit knockout.

🛡️ Resistance (−30)

If your Pokémon has a Resistance to a type, attacks from that type deal 30 less damage. A 100-damage attack becomes 70. Not all Pokémon have Resistance — many cards leave this blank. Weakness is applied first, then Resistance. Both only apply to Active Pokémon.

💡

Key rule: Weakness and Resistance are based on the attacking Pokémon's type, not the type of Energy used to pay for the attack. A Water-type Pokémon attacking with an attack that costs Colorless Energy still counts as a Water-type attack.

Chapter 10

Special Conditions (Status Effects)

Certain attacks and Abilities can inflict Special Conditions on a Pokémon, impairing its ability to fight. There are five conditions. Between each turn, there is a "Pokémon Checkup" phase where condition effects are resolved.

You can cure any Special Condition by: retreating to the Bench, evolving the Pokémon, or using certain Item/Ability effects.

☠️ Poisoned

Place a Poison marker on the Pokémon. During every Pokémon Checkup, it takes 1 damage counter (10 damage). Poison doesn't go away on its own — you must cure it.

Marker: green skull token

🔥 Burned

Place a Burn marker. During Checkup, put 2 damage counters (20 damage) on it, then flip a coin — heads cures the Burn, tails means it stays.

Marker: red/orange token

😴 Asleep

Turn the card sideways (counterclockwise). The Pokémon can't attack or retreat. During Checkup, flip a coin — heads wakes it up, tails stays Asleep.

Shown by: card rotated left

⚡ Paralyzed

Turn the card sideways (clockwise). The Pokémon can't attack or retreat. It auto-recovers at the end of its owner's next turn during Checkup.

Shown by: card rotated right

😵 Confused

Turn the card upside-down. When a Confused Pokémon tries to attack, flip a coin — heads means the attack proceeds normally, tails cancels the attack and the Pokémon takes 3 damage counters (30 damage) to itself.

Shown by: card flipped upside-down

Stacking Rules

Poisoned and Burned can coexist with each other and with any other condition. However, Asleep, Paralyzed, and Confused are mutually exclusive — they all use card rotation to indicate their status, so a new one replaces the old one. A Pokémon could theoretically be Poisoned, Burned, and Paralyzed all at once.

Chapter 11

How to Win the Game

There are three separate ways to win. You only need to achieve one of them:

🏆

Take All Prize Cards

Every time you Knock Out an opponent's Pokémon, you take one of your 6 Prize Cards. Take your sixth and final Prize Card, and you win. This is the most common path to victory.

💨

Wipe Out All Pokémon

If you Knock Out your opponent's Active Pokémon and they have no Benched Pokémon to replace it, you win immediately — even if you haven't taken all your Prizes yet.

📭

Deck Out Your Opponent

At the start of each turn, a player must draw a card. If their deck is empty and they can't draw, they lose. Running someone out of cards is a rarer but legitimate strategy.

Chapter 12

Quick Glossary

Key terms you'll hear during a game — tap any term to expand it:

Active Spot ▼
The position where your main battling Pokémon sits. You must always have one Pokémon in the Active Spot. Only the Active Pokémon can attack or be attacked (normally).
Bench ▼
The area behind the Active Spot where up to 5 additional Pokémon wait. Benched Pokémon can receive Energy and evolve, but they can't attack. When your Active Pokémon is Knocked Out, you promote one from the Bench.
Evolve ▼
Playing a higher-stage Pokémon card on top of a matching lower-stage one. For example, placing "Charmeleon" (Stage 1) on top of "Charmander" (Basic). Evolving keeps all attached Energy and damage counters but clears Special Conditions. You can't evolve a Pokémon on the same turn it was played or on the first turn of the game.
Knocked Out (KO) ▼
When a Pokémon has damage equal to or greater than its HP, it's Knocked Out. It and all cards attached go to the discard pile. The player who scored the KO takes a Prize Card.
Retreat ▼
Moving your Active Pokémon to the Bench and replacing it with a Benched one. You must discard Energy cards from the retreating Pokémon equal to its Retreat Cost (shown at the bottom of the card). You may only retreat once per turn.
Prize Cards ▼
The 6 face-down cards set aside during setup. You don't know what they are until you take them. Each KO earns you one Prize Card (some powerful Pokémon give up 2 or 3 when Knocked Out). Take your last Prize Card to win.
Pokémon Checkup ▼
A between-turns step after one player attacks but before the next player draws. During this step, Special Condition effects are resolved in order: Poisoned → Burned → Asleep → Paralyzed. Both players check their Pokémon during this phase.
Damage Counter ▼
A token (or die) placed on a Pokémon to track damage. Each counter represents 10 damage. When total counters × 10 equal or exceed a Pokémon's HP, it's Knocked Out. Dice are commonly used as counters in casual play.
Ability ▼
A special power printed on some Pokémon cards that provides an effect separate from attacks. Abilities may be activated once per turn, be always-on, or trigger under certain conditions. Unlike attacks, using an Ability doesn't end your turn.
Rule of Four ▼
You can only have up to 4 copies of any card with the same name in your deck — with the exception of Basic Energy cards, which have no limit. Special cards like ACE SPEC are limited to just 1 copy per deck.
